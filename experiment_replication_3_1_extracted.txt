       Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.07169  
        
       Von Arnim et al. (2024) present a system for simultaneously identifying and tracking moving 
       objects using blinking infrared beacons and an event based camera. The main problem they 
       address is that traditional frame based cameras are too slow to reliably decode high frequency 
       blinking signals, especially when the beacons are moving. Their solution combines the 
       microsecond level responsiveness of an event camera with a spiking neural network that the 
       system figures out which direction and how fast the beacon is moving, so it can keep following it 
       as it moves around for tracking, and decodes the beacon's blink pattern to recover a transmitted 
       ID number. The result is a system capable of both reading data from a beacon and following its 
       position in real time, which is something no prior method had achieved together. 
        
       The communication side of the system works by encoding an identification number into an 11-bit 
       sequence that the beacon repeatedly transmits by blinking on and off. The event camera picks 
       up each brightness transition as it happens, and the system reconstructs the bit sequence from 
       the timing of those transitions. Because event cameras react to changes rather than capturing 
       full frames, they can keep up with beacon frequencies far beyond what conventional cameras 
       allow. The authors tested this in a static indoor setup and found that their system maintained 
       over 94% message accuracy at frequencies up to 2.5 kHz, outperforming prior event based 
       approaches that topped out at 500 bps. 
        
       For the purposes of Section 3.1, figure 6 of this paper is something that I plan to replicate. It 
       plots Message Accuracy Rate (MAR) against increasing beacon frequency, showing where the 
       camera's temporal resolution begins to break down. This is directly comparable to 
       characterizing timestamp precision, minimum detectable pulse width, and decoding reliability, 
       which are the exact metrics the baseline evaluation targets.  
                                                  
        
       Because von Arnim et al. report their results in terms of Message Accuracy Rate rather than Bit 
       Error Rate, a numerical comparison requires conversion between the two metrics. Assuming bit 
       errors occur independently across a message, the probability that an entire message is 
       decoded correctly is the probability that every individual bit is correct, giving MAR = (1 - 
       BER)^N, where N is the message length in bits. Rearranging, BER = 1 - MAR^(1/N). For their 11 
       bit sequence, a reported MAR of 94% at 2.5 kHz corresponds to an equivalent BER of 
       approximately 0.6%, meaning around 6 out of every 1000 bits were in error. Replicating this 
       experiment serves as a validation of my own experimental setup, because if the EVK4 produces 
       BER results close to this 0.6% benchmark under equivalent modulation conditions, it confirms 
       that my hardware, decoding pipeline, and methodology are functioning correctly. This in turn 
       strengthens the validity of the subsequent OOK and CSK results collected throughout the rest of 
       the thesis.
