             1.1 INTRODUCTION 
                   Visible light communication (VLC) is emerging as a low cost, low interference alternative to 
             radio frequency (RF) communication for indoor Internet of Things (IoT) and robotics. But current VLC 
             receivers, photodiodes and rolling shutter cameras consume significant power, struggle with fast 
             modulation, and break down under real world lighting variations. Event driven cameras may solve these 
             challenges, but their performance as VLC receivers remains underexplored. 
                   Event driven cameras record only changes in brightness instead of continuously capturing full 
             image frames. By ignoring static visual information, they react to light changes almost instantly and 
             consume much less energy than conventional rolling shutter cameras [3]. These properties make them 
             promising receivers for VLC, a wireless system where LEDs transmit data by rapidly modulating their 
             brightness [1]. This research goes into how effectively event driven cameras can decode VLC signals 
             from blinking LEDs and whether they offer advantages over traditional cameras in latency (ms), energy 
             per bit (J/bit), and decoding accuracy measured using bit error rate (BER). By testing on-off keying 
             (OOK) under both line-of-sight (LOS) and limited non-line-of-sight (NLOS) conditions, and by changing 
             ambient illumination measured in lux using an external light meter, the study evaluates the potential of 
             event based sensors as practical, low energy optical receivers [2]. Additionally, this research will explore 
             the possibilities of color shift keying (CSK) with event driven cameras, with three potential schemes to do 
             so. The results aim to determine whether event driven cameras can support future VLC systems for 
             robotics, IoT devices, and neuromorphic communication technologies.  
              
             2.1 BACKGROUND 
                   VLC enables wireless data transmission through rapid modulation of LED brightness, offering a 
             low cost and interference free alternative to RF systems. Recent work has demonstrated that event based 
             optical sensors can decode VLC signals by responding to rapid brightness changes rather than full image 
             frames [2, 3, 4]. This proposal investigates whether event driven cameras, whose pixels independently 
             report changes in brightness, can serve as efficient low power receivers for VLC. 
                   Prior studies have shown that VLC can achieve high speed and energy efficient communication 
             using temporal modulation (OOK) or multi-chromatic encoding schemes such as CSK [4]. The issue is 
             that most existing VLC receivers rely on photodiodes or traditional rolling shutter cameras. These sensors 
             need continuous frame capture or high rate sampling, resulting in high power consumption. They also 
             suffer from motion blur, limited sampling speed, and severe degradation under uncontrolled ambient 
                 SENIOR THESIS PROPOSAL - CHIP BISHOP, UCSC ELECTRICAL ENGINEERING                                              2 
                 lighting conditions [2, 6]. Event driven cameras provide an appealing alternative because they output 
                 asynchronous per pixel events with microsecond resolution, capturing only dynamic brightness changes 
                 while ignoring static information. This sparse and low latency sensing architecture suggests that event 
                 cameras may support robust, low energy VLC reception ideal for embedded and mobile systems. 
                          Several studies demonstrate that event driven optical sensors can decode OOK based VLC by 
                 detecting abrupt brightness transitions in modulated LEDs [8, 11]. These systems report timestamped 
                 events corresponding to logical transitions and achieve reliable decoding under controlled illumination. In 
                 contrast, current literature on event camera VLC rarely evaluates multi chromatic modulation such as 
                 CSK. Traditional CMOS based CSK systems have been demonstrated using RGB LED mixtures and 
                 chromaticity mapping, achieving reduced BER compared to OOK under illumination fluctuations [10]. 
                 The only known work applying color processing to event-based sensors [9] focused on asynchronous 
                 color segmentation for vision tasks rather than modulated light communication. No prior study has 
                 examined whether event sensors can recover chromaticity information reliably enough to support CSK 
                 within a VLC system. Given that the only instance of CSK applied to event based sensors was not 
                 conducted within a VLC framework, a research gap exists regarding whether event cameras can recover 
                 chromaticity information with sufficient reliability to enable CSK in visible light communication. In 
                 addition to the lack of CSK focused studies, existing work on event-based VLC is often limited to 
                 controlled illumination conditions.  
                          Existing event based VLC work mostly evaluates decoding under heavily controlled lighting, 
                 where ambient illumination, shadows, reflections, and NLOS effects are absent or intentionally 
                 minimized. Real indoor environments often introduce fluctuating illumination, partial occlusion, and 
                 reflective surfaces that can disrupt the detection of LED intensity changes and increase BER, yet no 
                 studies investigate how event cameras perform under these realistic conditions. Prior work rarely 
                 measures energy-per-bit (J/bit) or provides direct comparisons against rolling shutter smartphone cameras, 
                 leaving open the question of whether event cameras offer a meaningful energy advantage for VLC [8, 10].  
                          To address these gaps, this proposal systematically evaluates event driven cameras under both 
                 controlled and realistic indoor lighting, quantifying performance using BER, latency, and energy per bit 
                 across OOK and CSK modulation schemes. By explicitly measuring decoding reliability under varying 
                 lux levels, shadows, reflections, and limited NLOS conditions, this study aims to determine whether event 
                 cameras can function as practical, robust VLC receivers that work for embedded, robotic, and 
                 neuromorphic communication systems.

                  3.0 EXPERIMENTAL OBJECTIVES AND EVALUATION FRAMEWORK 
                           This section outlines a structured experimental framework to evaluate the usability of event 
                  driven cameras for embedded and optical communication systems. Because event sensors capture only 
                  brightness changes with microsecond level latency, they may offer advantages over conventional frame 
                  based CMOS cameras in decoding accuracy, energy efficiency, and resilience to ambient illumination.  
                           Section 3.1 investigates baseline event camera timing performance and decoding reliability. 
                  Section 3.2 quantifies total energy cost per bit to determine whether event-based sensing provides 
                  measurable reductions in power consumption. Section 3.3 evaluates robustness under real-world 
                  illumination conditions. Finally, Section 3.4 isolates and extends the evaluations from 3.1 and 3.2 to VLC 
                  experiments that consist of CSK. Section 3.4 will be done in parallel to 3.1 and 3.2, as shown in figure 2, 
                  in order to allow flexibility with beginning section 3.3 with OOK experiments separately from 3.4. The 
                  CSK experiments represent a relatively unexplored area of study, and it is uncertain whether they will 
                  yield measurable results such as quantifiable bit error rates, symbol decoding accuracy, or energy-per-bit 
                  performance within the project timeline. Separating the CSK experiments into their own phase ensures 
                  that potential delays or inconclusive outcomes from this exploratory work will not impede progress on 
                  Section 3.3. Together, these studies determine whether event-driven sensors can serve as practical, 
                  low-power receivers for embedded and optical wireless communication.  
                           In all experiments, a predefined binary sequence will be transmitted using the LED array through 
                  OOK or CSK modulation. The EVK4 event camera will record asynchronous brightness change events, 
                  which will be grouped into fixed symbol intervals to reconstruct the transmitted bitstream. The recovered 
                  sequence will then be compared to the known transmitted sequence to compute BER. 
                  3.1 BASELINE EVENT CAMERA PERFORMANCE EVALUATION 
                           The first objective is to characterize the intrinsic timing precision and responsiveness of the event 
                  camera under controlled optical conditions. This establishes a foundation for later VLC experiments. Key 
                  metrics include timestamp precision and latency, evaluated through controlled brightness transitions. It 
                  also includes inter-event arrival spacing and minimum detectable pulse width, determining temporal 
                  resolution limits. Finally, we will conduct event density and signal-to-noise ratio under varying 
                  illumination. 
                           This baseline testing verifies that the event camera can capture microsecond-scale transitions 
             SENIOR THESIS PROPOSAL - CHIP BISHOP, UCSC ELECTRICAL ENGINEERING                  5 
             consistently and that timestamp jitter remains below the modulation symbol period. These results will 
             define how well the camera performs on its own, before testing any specific modulation schemes.  
                    
             3.2 QUANTIFYING ENERGY EFFICIENCY FOR EMBEDDED DEVICES 
                   The second objective evaluates the total energy cost per bit (J/bit) for event-based sensing and 
             decoding compared to frame-based approaches. Experiments will measure sensing energy (camera power 
             consumption), compute energy (decoding and processing cost), and total energy per transmitted bit across 
             varying data rates and illumination levels. By avoiding frame capture and reconstruction, event cameras 
             are expected to reduce both sensing and computation energy. Demonstrating significantly lower J/bit 
             would establish them as efficient receivers for battery-limited IoT and robotic systems, even before 
             applying specific VLC modulation schemes. 
              
              
             3.3 IMPROVING ROBUSTNESS UNDER REAL-WORLD LIGHTING CONDITIONS 
             The third objective investigates how ambient illumination, shadows, reflections, and partial NLOS 
             visibility affect the reliability of bit recovery from event streams. Tests will vary lux levels, measured via 
             an external light meter, LOS and NLOS configurations, and artificial and natural lighting sources to 
             simulate uncontrolled indoor conditions. 
                   Performance will be quantified through BER, event timing stability, and frame drop analysis. 
             Because event cameras respond only to brightness changes, not absolute intensity, they are expected to 
             maintain lower BER under fluctuating illumination. If validated, this demonstrates their suitability for 
             practical VLC in dynamic indoor environments. 
                    
             3.4 VISIBLE LIGHT COMMUNICATION AND COLOR SHIFT KEYING EXPERIMENTS 
                   The fourth objective extends the experiments from Sections 3.1 and 3.2 into a CSK context and is 
             conducted at the end of Winter 2026 and throughout Spring 2026. These experiments run in parallel with 
             baseline timing and energy-efficiency evaluations to allow exploratory CSK development without 
             delaying the robustness studies in Section 3.3. While the previous sections evaluate timing precision, 
             decoding reliability, and energy efficiency under intensity-based modulation (OOK), this section repeats 
             those same analyses using color-encoded modulation (CSK). The goal is to determine whether event 
             driven cameras can maintain comparable temporal accuracy, decoding performance, and energy-per-bit 
             efficiency when processing multi-chromatic VLC signals. 
                   LED transmitters modulate intensity or color to encode binary and multilevel data symbols. Event 
             timestamps will be decoded to recover transmitted bit sequences. Key evaluation metrics include BER 
             under different bitrates and illumination levels, timing accuracy and event to symbol alignment, and 
             minimum detectable pulse width and latency variance. The goal is to keep the BER below the Forward 
             Error Correction threshold (5–10 kbit/s), consistent with previous rolling-shutter VLC results [5, 6]. 
             SENIOR THESIS PROPOSAL - CHIP BISHOP, UCSC ELECTRICAL ENGINEERING                  6 
                   Three event-based modulation schemes will be implemented. First is spatially separated RGB 
             LEDs (red -> right, green -> center, blue -> left). Another is frequency-encoded channels, where each 
             color toggles at a unique frequency (e.g., red 400 Hz, green 800 Hz, blue 1200 Hz). Another is 
             brightness-ratio encoding, mapping intensity ratios to color states (strong = red, medium = green, weak = 
             blue). These configurations test whether event sensors can recover chromaticity information (analogous to 
             CSK) reliably enough for multi-channel VLC. Results will compare BER, symbol timing accuracy, and 
             energy per bit across schemes.

             4.1 METHODOLOGY INTRODUCTION 
                   How will this research be conducted? This research will be conducted in two phases: controlled 
             laboratory experiments and real world environmental evaluation. Both phases investigate VLC 
             performance using an event driven camera as the receiver. Each stage measures decoding accuracy, 
             latency, and energy per bit using different modulation schemes, lighting conditions, and LOS 
             configurations. The main equipment used will consist of the EVK4 event camera, and laptop with the 
             Prophesee Metavision software. Other equipment includes a lux meter, power meter, Google Pixel 7a, 
             BeagleBoneBlack, and an RGB LED array.

             4.2 LABORATORY EXPERIMENTS 
                   The laboratory phase will establish a controlled VLC environment to evaluate OOK and CSK 
             under varying optical conditions. An RGB LED array driven by a programmable microcontroller, a 
             BeagleBoneBlack, will transmit binary data sequences. Ambient illumination will be systematically 
             adjusted using a dimmable light source, and measured with a calibrated external lux meter to quantify 
             noise levels. The event driven camera will record asynchronous brightness changes generated by LED 
             modulation, and the resulting data stream will be decoded into bit sequences using event timestamps. 
             Power consumption of the camera and the decoding system will be measured using a power meter and 
             normalized to energy per bit (J/bit). These experiments determine baseline BER, latency, and energy 
             characteristics under known lighting thresholds. 
                    
             4.3 REAL WORLD INDOOR EVALUATIONS 
                   Upon completion of laboratory testing, the same setup will be evaluated in real world indoor 
             environments. Testing will be performed in different rooms throughout the Westside Research Park, a 
             hallway with mixed natural and artificial illumination, and locations that introduce partial obstruction for 
             limited NLOS scenarios. Lighting levels will again be measured using a lux meter to correlate ambient 
             noise and BER. The experimental objective is to quantify how lighting fluctuations, shadowing, and 
             reflections influence decoding reliability of OOK for event based VLC receivers. Power consumption will 
             be monitored to ensure that low energy performance persists in non controlled and random environments. 
                    
                    
             4.4 COMPARISON AGAINST CONVENTIONAL CAMERA 
                   Finally, results from the event driven camera will be compared against those obtained using a 
             conventional rolling shutter CMOS camera, a Google Pixel 7a, under identical lighting and modulation 
             conditions. The comparison will include BER, latency, and energy per bit measurements, enabling an 
             evaluation of the benefits and limitations of event driven sensors in VLC systems. This methodology 
             SENIOR THESIS PROPOSAL - CHIP BISHOP, UCSC ELECTRICAL ENGINEERING                  8 
             ensures that performance claims are both empirically validated and representative of real indoor 
             communication conditions encountered by robotics, IoT sensors, and low power optical networks. 
                    
                    
             5.1 TIMELINE 
                   Work on this project began in Fall 2025 with setup and calibration of the Prophesee EVK4 event 
             camera. During this phase, I activated and installed the necessary software to enable working with the 
             EVK4 camera, and established a working proficiency with the operation of the camera. Additionally, all 
             necessary measuring and experimental tools for future experiments were collected and accounted for.  
             In Winter 2026, the focus shifts to full VLC decoding using the EVK4, including reconstruction of OOK 
             and CSK signals from event streams and measurement of BER under different bitrates, LED drive 
             conditions, and illumination levels. The objective of this phase is to demonstrate reliable bit extraction 
             and generate BER results that can be compared to published rolling shutter CMOS benchmarks. 
             Spring 2026 will quantify total energy cost per bit for event based reception by measuring sensing and 
             compute energy under matched communication settings. This produces a direct comparison between 
             sparse event capture and frame-based processing, establishing how much energy can be saved when using 
             an event camera as the VLC receiver. 
             Finally, in Fall 2026, VLC performance will be evaluated under realistic indoor lighting, including 
             varying measured illuminance, shadows, reflections, mixed light sources, and limited NLOS. These tests 
             determine whether event cameras maintain acceptable BER and timing fidelity in environments where 
             conventional CMOS sensors often fail. Results from all phases will be consolidated in the senior thesis 
             submitted in mid-December 2026. 
             6.1 CONCLUSION 
                   This research proposes a low cost, energy efficient VLC system that uses event driven cameras as 
             optical receivers. By taking advantage of their asynchronous and low power sensing architecture, this 
             work seeks to show that event cameras can reliably decode data transmitted by modulated LEDs in both 
             controlled and real world indoor environments. The study investigates performance under variable 
             lighting, compares results against conventional rolling shutter cameras, and evaluates communication 
             performance using metrics such as bit error rate, latency, and energy per bit.  
                   The results of this project have the potential to broaden the role of event driven imaging systems 
             further than vision only applications and into low power wireless communication. If successful, event 
             cameras could offer a new path for optical communication in battery constrained devices, enabling 
             smarter networks of robots, autonomous sensors in agricultural settings, and IoT systems that 
             communicate using ambient light. By reducing power consumption while maintaining reliable data 
             transmission, this work contributes to the development of sustainable and efficient communication 
             technologies for future embedded and neuromorphic systems.
